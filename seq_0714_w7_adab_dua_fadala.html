The Ādāb of Duʿāʾ · 1

“This Man Has Hurried” — Praise, Then Ṣalāt, Then Ask

The ḥadīth of Faḍāla ibn ʿUbayd

The Messenger of Allah ﷺ heard a man supplicate in his prayer without praising Allah or sending ṣalāt upon the Prophet. He ﷺ said: “This one has hurried.” Then he called him and said: “إِذَا صَلَّى أَحَدُكُمْ فَلْيَبْدَأْ بِتَحْمِيدِ رَبِّهِ عَزَّ وَجَلَّ وَالثَّنَاءِ عَلَيْهِ، ثُمَّ يُصَلِّي عَلَى النَّبِيِّ ﷺ، ثُمَّ يَدْعُو بَعْدُ بِمَا شَاءَ — When one of you prays, let him begin by praising his Lord and extolling Him, then send ṣalāt upon the Prophet ﷺ, then ask after that for whatever he wishes.” (Abū Dāwūd, al-Tirmidhī — ḥasan ṣaḥīḥ; ṣaḥḥaḥahu Ibn Ḥibbān and al-Ḥākim.)

This is the master-pattern, and its why is plain: praise, then ṣalāt on the Prophet ﷺ, then the request. A ṣalāt upon him ﷺ is never turned back, and the scholars said a duʿāʾ placed between two accepted ṣalawāt is not left suspended between them. So the praise and the ṣalāt are not a preamble to get through. They are what carries the request up.

In practice

“Hurrying” today rarely looks like rudeness; it looks like a believer who, the instant the imām says the salām, plunges straight into “Yā Rabb, the rent, the result, the marriage…” The adab is to take ten unhurried seconds first — الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ… اللَّهُمَّ صَلِّ عَلَى مُحَمَّدٍ — and only then bring the need. The need loses nothing by waiting; it gains a doorway.
