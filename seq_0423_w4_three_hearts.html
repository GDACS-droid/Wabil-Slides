Anwāʿ al-Qulūb

Dark, Contested, Radiant

A heart that appetite has conquered and hawā has captured cannot escape the whisperings. So Ibn al-Qayyim sorts the hearts into three.
  • A heart empty of faith and all good — a dark heart (قَلْبٌ مُظْلِمٌ). Shayṭān rests from casting whispers into it, for he has already taken it as a home and a homeland and rules it however he wishes.
  • A heart lit by the light of faith, its lamp kindled — but over it lie the darkness of appetites and the gales of passions. There shayṭān has advance and retreat, designs and ambitions; the war is by turns (دُوَلٌ وسِجَالٌ), sometimes his and sometimes the slave's.
  • A heart filled with faith, its lamp ablaze, the veils of appetite drawn back. Its light has a radiance and the radiance a burning: were the whisper to draw near, it would be scorched. It is like a sky guarded by stars — if shayṭān approached to cross it, he would be driven off by a flame.

The sky is no more sacred than the believer's heart, and Allah's guarding of it is more complete than His guarding of the sky. The sky is the place of the angels' worship and the descent of revelation; the believer's heart is the seat of tawḥīd, love, knowledge, and faith — so it is fit to be guarded from the enemy's plot, taken only by a momentary heedlessness that no human is wholly free of.
