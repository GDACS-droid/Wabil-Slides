The Muqaddima

A Sin to Paradise, a Good Deed to the Fire

The saying of the salaf, told in full

Here Ibn al-Qayyim turns the whole matter of sin on its head with a word of the salaf: إِنَّ العَبْدَ لَيَعْمَلُ الذَّنْبَ يَدْخُلُ بِهِ الجَنَّةَ، وَيَعْمَلُ الحَسَنَةَ يَدْخُلُ بِهَا النَّارَ — “a servant may commit a sin and enter Paradise by it, and do a good deed and enter the Fire by it.” How? The sin: it stays before his eyes, so he is fearful, ashamed, weeping, regretful, his head bowed before his Lord, his heart broken — until that one sin, by the brokenness it brings, becomes أَنْفَعَ لَهُ مِنْ طَاعَاتٍ كَثِيرَةٍ, more profitable to him than many acts of obedience. The good deed: he keeps reminding Allah of it, growing proud, swelling, saying “I did, I did,” until the ʿujb and arrogance it breeds become the cause of his ruin.

The hinge

So an act is not weighed only as an act. A sin met with a broken heart can lift a man; a virtue met with self-admiration can sink him. The editor traces this meaning to the early generations — narrated of al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī and others, and recorded by Aḥmad and Ibn al-Mubārak in their books of al-Zuhd.
