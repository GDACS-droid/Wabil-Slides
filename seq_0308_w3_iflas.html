Bāb al-Iflās

No Veil Thicker Than a Claim

Ibn al-Qayyim presses the two wings to their end. The nearest door by which a servant enters upon his Lord, he says, is بَابُ الإِفْلَاسِ — the door of bankruptcy: that he sees in himself no state, no station, no means he leans on, no merit he holds over Allah. He comes from الاِفْتِقَارِ الصِّرْفِ — sheer need — like one whose poverty has cracked his heart to its core.

Servitude rests on two foundations

He states the principle plainly: لَا طَرِيقَ إِلَى اللَّهِ أَقْرَبُ مِنَ العُبُودِيَّةِ، وَلَا حِجَابَ أَغْلَظُ مِنَ الدَّعْوَى — no road to Allah is nearer than servitude, and no veil is thicker than a claim. And العُبُودِيَّةُ مَدَارُهَا عَلَى قَاعِدَتَيْنِ: حُبٌّ كَامِلٌ وَذُلٌّ تَامٌّ — servitude turns on complete love and total lowliness, the very fruits of the two wings. The thickest barrier between the servant and his Lord is the inner whisper, I have something to show.
