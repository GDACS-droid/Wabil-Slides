The Muqaddima

When Loves Collide, Which One Rules?

The heart stands straight, he says, by two things. The first is that the love of Allah outranks and governs every other love — not merely that it is present, but that when two loves collide, it wins.
وَمَا أَسْهَلَ هَذَا بِالدَّعْوَى، وَمَا أَصْعَبَهُ بِالفِعْلِ! فَعِنْدَ الامْتِحَانِ يُكْرَمُ المَرْءُ أَوْ يُهَانُ
“How easy this is as a claim, and how hard in the doing! For it is at the moment of testing that a man is honored or disgraced.”
wa-mā ashala hādhā bi-d-daʿwā, wa-mā aṣʿabahu bi-l-fiʿl! fa-ʿinda l-imtiḥāni yukramu l-marʾu aw yuhān
Ibn al-Qayyim, al-Wābil al-Ṣayyib
In practice

He names the failure exactly: how often a servant puts what he craves, or what his elder, or his chief, or his shaykh, or his family wants, ahead of what Allah loves. The Fajr alarm against the warmth of sleep; the halal income against the lucrative deal that needs one lie; the sunna against what the people you admire will think. Daʿwā says the love of Allah comes first; imtiḥān is the alarm at 5 a.m. And the author warns of the consequence: it is Allah's sunna that whoever puts another's love above His has that very thing made bitter to him — مَنْ أَحَبَّ شَيْئًا سِوَاهُ عُذِّبَ بِهِ: whoever loves a thing besides Allah is punished by the very thing he loved.
