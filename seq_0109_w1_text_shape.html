The Text

A Letter on the Heart — and the Road of the Course

  • What it is: not a treatise but a letter to a friend — the manuscript even preserves the author's own first name for it, al-Kalim al-Ṭayyib wa-l-ʿamal al-ṣāliḥ. It is intimate, urgent, written to be acted on.
  • Its spine — the heart: when it is sound the limbs are sound, and when it is corrupt so is all that stands on it. Tonight's muqaddima lays the heart's foundations.
  • Its center of gravity — dhikr: its merits, its many forms, and the long count of its fawāʾid (benefits) — which we will take one by one in the later weeks.
  • Its close — the wird: the prophetic adhkār themselves, arranged as a working morning-and-evening practice that this course will end by taking on.
Today

So treat this differently from a book you only finish. Read the assigned matn before each session, sit with the Arabic of the proofs, and — the part most people skip — act on one thing a week. A man who has read every fawāʾid of dhikr and added none to his day has missed the genre: this was a letter telling a friend what to do.
