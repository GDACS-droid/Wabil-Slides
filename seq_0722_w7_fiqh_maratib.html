A Most Useful Principle

When the Lesser Act Becomes the Better in Its Place

Ibn al-Qayyim calls this “a most useful principle” (أَصْلٌ نَافِعٌ جِدًّا) — a فِقْه of the self that separates the excellence of a thing in itself from its excellence in its proper place. The more excellent act, done out of its place, becomes a loss:
  • In رُكُوع and سُجُود, تَسْبِيح outranks recitation — indeed reciting the Qurʾān there is forbidden; each posture has its appointed word.
  • Answering the مُؤَذِّن, and the ذِكْر fixed after the prayer — تَهْلِيل, تَسْبِيح, تَكْبِير, تَحْمِيد — outrank a general recitation in that very moment.
  • If a fear of the شَيَاطِين presses, the protective أَذْكَار are better there than open recitation; if a thought of one's sins arises, turning to تَوْبَة and اِسْتِغْفَار is better.
  • If an urgent need keeps the heart from presence in recitation, then turning to ask Allah for it — with a heart fully gathered (اجْتَمَعَ قَلْبُهُ كُلُّهُ عَلَى اللَّه) — may be the most beneficial of all right then.
