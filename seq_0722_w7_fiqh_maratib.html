A Most Useful Principle

When the Lesser Act Becomes the Better in Its Place

Ibn al-Qayyim calls this “a most useful principle” (أَصْلٌ نَافِعٌ جِدًّا) — a fiqh of the self that separates the excellence of a thing in itself from its excellence in its proper place. The more excellent act, done out of its place, becomes a loss:
  • In rukūʿ and sujūd, tasbīḥ outranks recitation — indeed reciting the Qurʾān there is forbidden; each posture has its appointed word.
  • Answering the muʾadhdhin, and the dhikr fixed after the prayer — tahlīl, tasbīḥ, takbīr, taḥmīd — outrank a general recitation in that very moment.
  • If a fear of the shayāṭīn presses, the protective adhkār are better there than open recitation; if a thought of one's sins arises, turning to tawba and istighfār is better.
  • If an urgent need keeps the heart from presence in recitation, then turning to ask Allah for it — with a heart fully gathered (اجْتَمَعَ قَلْبُهُ كُلُّهُ عَلَى اللَّه) — may be the most beneficial of all right then.
