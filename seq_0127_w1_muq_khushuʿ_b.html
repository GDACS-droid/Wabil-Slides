The Muqaddima

A Prayer Without خُشُوع — a Body Without a Soul

What the servant keeps of his prayer

A prayer empty of خُشُوع and presence of heart, he says, is كَبَدَنٍ مَيِّتٍ لَا رُوحَ فِيهِ — like a dead body with no soul in it. Would a man dare to gift a dead slave to a king and expect it received with honor? لَيْسَ لِلْعَبْدِ مِنْ صَلَاتِهِ إِلَّا مَا عَقَلَ مِنْهَا — “a servant has of his prayer only what he was conscious of.” This is the proof, in the prayer itself, of the claim we began with: the same prayer, weighed by two hearts, comes back half-full or whole.

Today

Think of the عِشَاء you prayed last night. If your body stood and bowed while your mind drafted tomorrow's emails and replayed an argument — and you reached the تَسْلِيم without having once registered a word you said — that is the prayer Ibn al-Qayyim calls a body with no soul: the obligation is lifted in this world's reckoning, but of its reward you keep only the few moments your heart was actually present. The remedy is not to wait for a feeling to descend. Understand what you recite, and when the mind bolts, gently bring it back — even a few sentences truly attended to are written for you, while a flawless recitation the heart slept through is not.
