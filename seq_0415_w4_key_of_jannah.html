تَوْحِيد

A Key — and Its Teeth

Because شِرْك is the gravest of the three ledgers, the Garden is forbidden to its people: no soul of شِرْك enters it, and only the people of تَوْحِيد do. تَوْحِيد is the key to its door, and no door opens to a key without teeth (مِفْتَاحٌ لَا أَسْنَانَ لَهُ).

The teeth are the obligations

The teeth Ibn al-Qayyim names are the prayer, fasting, زَكَاة, حَجّ, جِهَاد, enjoining good and forbidding evil, truthful speech, returning trusts, keeping ties of kin, and dutifulness to parents. Whoever takes in this world a sound key of تَوْحِيد and sets in it teeth of obedience comes on the Day of Resurrection to the door of the Garden, and nothing bars him from opening it — unless sins remain whose trace تَوْبَة and اِسْتِغْفَار did not remove here, so he is held back until he is purified.

A corollary

This answers a real anxiety. “لَا إِلَٰهَ إِلَّا اللَّٰه” is the key. But a key filed smooth, a tongue that affirms while the limbs render nothing, opens nothing. And the obligations without the key — works without تَوْحِيد, done for show or for other than Allah — are teeth with no key to set them in. You need both in the same hand: the word and the works it commits you to.
