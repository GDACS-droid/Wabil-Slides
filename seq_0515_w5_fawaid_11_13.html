Fawāʾid al-Dhikr

It Brings Him Back, Brings Him Near, Opens the Door

  • 11 — It bequeaths إِنَابَة, the turning back. يُورِثُهُ الْإِنَابَةَ. The more a servant returns to Allah with his tongue, the more his heart learns to return to Him in every state — so that Allah becomes, in Ibn al-Qayyim’s words, مَفْزَعَهُ وَمَلْجَأَهُ، وَمَلَاذَهُ وَمَعَاذَهُ: his refuge, his shelter, his sanctuary, and the place he flees to when calamity falls. The reflex of the heart under sudden pressure is built by what the tongue did in calm.
  • 12 — It bequeaths nearness (الْقُرْب). The measure is exact, and he states it as a law: عَلَى قَدْرِ ذِكْرِهِ لِلَّهِ يَكُونُ قُرْبُهُ مِنْهُ، وَعَلَى قَدْرِ غَفْلَتِهِ يَكُونُ بُعْدُهُ مِنْهُ — as much as a servant remembers Allah, that much is his nearness; as much as he is heedless, that much his distance. There is no nearness on offer that bypasses remembrance.
  • 13 — It opens a vast door of مَعْرِفَة. كُلَّمَا أَكْثَرَ مِنَ الذِّكْرِ ازْدَادَ مِنَ الْمَعْرِفَةِ. ذِكْر is not the rival of عِلْم but one of its springs: the more a servant remembers, the more his knowledge of Allah grows — and Benefit 14 follows from it, the awe (الْهَيْبَة) of a heart so filled with His presence that reverence settles into it.
