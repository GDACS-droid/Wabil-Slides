The Author

Sixteen Years — and a Shared Cell

  • The teacher who shaped him most was Ibn Taymiyya, whom he accompanied for some sixteen years — from the shaykh's return to Damascus from Egypt in 712H until his death in 728H.
  • He did not only study with his shaykh; he shared his trials. In the persecution of 726H, Ibn al-Qayyim was confined with him in the citadel of Damascus, and was not released until after Ibn Taymiyya died there in 728H.
  • What he did in confinement tells you the man: he gave himself to the Qurʾān, reciting and reflecting on it. That is the ʿubūdiyya in tribulation this book is about, lived out — he met the decree by turning to his Lord, not by raging against it.
A corollary

Notice what the biography quietly proves: the author is not lecturing us from comfort. When he writes, a few pages on, that hardship is sent لِيَمْتَحِنَ صَبْرَهُ وَعُبُودِيَّتَهُ — to test a servant's patience and servanthood — he is describing a road he himself walked, imprisoned beside his teacher. We read his words on ṣabr as testimony, not theory.
