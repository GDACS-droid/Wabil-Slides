Benefit 73 · The Climactic Fruit

The Fortress: Dhikr Drives the Shayāṭīn Away

The seventy-third, which Ibn al-Qayyim opened the book with

He saves the greatest for last, the one “we began the book by mentioning,” now laid out in full for its vast benefit and every soul's need of it: the shayāṭīn have surrounded the servant; they are his enemies, hemming him in, each striking at him with whatever harm it can. وَلَا سَبِيلَ إِلَى تَفْرِيقِ جَمْعِهِمْ عَنْهُ إِلَّا بِذِكْرِ اللَّه — “and there is no way to break up their host from around him except by the remembrance of Allah.” This is the meaning of the ḥadīth of al-Ḥārith al-Ashʿarī taught earlier: Allah commanded dhikr, “and its likeness is a man whose enemy comes after him at speed, until he reaches a fortified stronghold and shields himself inside it” — and from the shayṭān a man shields himself only inside the fortress of dhikr.
Three walls, named precisely

Abū Khallād gave the measure: “Whoever enters Islam has entered one fortress; whoever enters the mosque has entered two; whoever sits in a circle remembering Allah has entered three.” And the Prophet ﷺ taught the door-prayer of the fourth wall — “when one of you leaves his house and says بِسْمِ اللَّهِ، تَوَكَّلْتُ عَلَى اللَّهِ، لَا حَوْلَ وَلَا قُوَّةَ إِلَّا بِاللَّه, it is said to him: you are sufficed, guided and guarded, and the shayṭān turns aside” (al-Tirmidhī, ḥasan). So the protection is not a feeling; it is masonry you can name — your Islam, your prayer-place, your circle, and the words on your lips at the threshold.
