The Struggle

Besieged from Within

Ibn al-Qayyim says the slave was tested with appetite (الشَّهْوَة), anger (الغَضَب), and heedlessness (الغَفْلَة) — and with an enemy, Iblīs, who never tires of him.

Why the enemy is not a stranger

شَيْطَان does not break in from outside; he enters through doors that are already in your own self and nature. He comes carrying what you love, so the نَفْس leans toward him of its own accord. Then three agree against the slave — شَيْطَان, the النَّفْس الأَمَّارَة, and هَوَى — and become three commanding masters (ثَلَاثَةٌ مُسَلَّطُونَ آمِرُونَ). The limbs are only a tool that obeys: they go where they are sent and aim where they are pointed. So the limbs stay in their service, doing as they are ordered.

Today

This is why temptation so often arrives wearing the face of something you already wanted, speaking in your own voice rather than a stranger's: the phone reaching for your hand before فَجْر, the second look that calls itself harmless, the grudge that calls itself dignity. The enemy rarely argues; he simply hands you what the نَفْس was already half-reaching for.
