The Three Modes of Praise

How Allah Answers Each Line of the Opening

الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ ۝ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ ۝ مَالِكِ يَوْمِ الدِّينِ
“All praise is for Allah, Lord of the worlds; the Most Merciful, the Bestower of Mercy; Master of the Day of Judgement.”
Sūrat al-Fātiḥa 1:2–4

In the ḥadīth qudsī (Muslim), Allah divides the prayer between Himself and His servant: at الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ, “My servant has praised Me (ḥamidanī)”; at الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ, “My servant has extolled Me (athnā ʿalayya)”; at مَالِكِ يَوْمِ الدِّينِ, “My servant has glorified Me (majjadanī).”

Ibn al-Qayyim distinguishes the three: ḥamd is to report His perfections with love and contentment — the silent admirer is not a praiser, nor is the one who lists His attributes with no love. Thanāʾ is that praise repeated, attribute after attribute. And majd is praise of His majesty, greatness and dominion. So before you ask, you have three rising rungs of speech to climb.
