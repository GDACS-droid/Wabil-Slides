Muḥbiṭāt al-Aʿmāl

What Cancels a Deed

Ibn al-Qayyim warns: وَمُحْبِطَاتُ الأَعْمَالِ وَمُفْسِدَاتُهَا أَكْثَرُ مِنْ أَنْ تُحْصَرَ — the things that void and corrupt deeds are too many to count. The matter is not in the doing of the deed, but in guarding it from what spoils it.
  • رِيَاء — showing off: even a subtle trace of it voids the deed, and its doors are beyond counting.
  • A deed not bound to the سُنَّة in its form is likewise void, however earnest.
  • المَنّ — reminding Allah of your deed, or reminding people of your charity, corrupts it (al-Baqara 2:264).
إِنَّ العَبْدَ لَيَعْمَلُ العَمَلَ سِرًّا لَا يَطَّلِعُ عَلَيْهِ إِلَّا اللَّهُ، فَيَتَحَدَّثُ بِهِ، فَيُنْقَلُ مِنْ دِيوَانِ السِّرِّ إِلَى دِيوَانِ العَلَانِيَةِ، ثُمَّ يَصِيرُ فِي ذَلِكَ الدِّيوَانِ عَلَى حَسَبِ العَلَانِيَةِ.
“A servant does a deed in secret that none knows but Allah; then he speaks of it, and it is moved from the register of the secret to the register of the public — and then it stands in that register according to [the rule of] the public.”
A well-known athar Ibn al-Qayyim cites here; he notes its chains are weak — quoted for its meaning, that craving people's notice can void a once-pure deed
