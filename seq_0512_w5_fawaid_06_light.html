It gives light to the face and to the heart

يُنَوِّرُ الْوَجْهَ وَالْقَلْبَ. Dhikr is nūr, one of Ibn al-Qayyim’s great themes. A heart lit by remembrance reads reality rightly, seeing truth as truth and falsehood as falsehood; and that inner light does not stay hidden. It surfaces, until it shows on the face, on the limbs, and (he says) even on a person’s bearing among people. Its absence is darkness: a heart left unlit mistakes the false for the true. Clustered here are Benefits 5, 7, and 8: dhikr also strengthens the heart and body for action, draws down provision (rizq), and clothes the dhākir in awe (mahāba), sweetness (ḥalāwa), and a radiant freshness (naḍra) that others sense before a word is spoken.

أَنَّهُ يُنَوِّرُ الْوَجْهَ وَالْقَلْبَ
In practice

We have all met it without naming it: a person whose face simply carries something settled and clear, that no grooming explains and no hardship dims. Ibn al-Qayyim is saying that light is not cosmetic. It is the overflow of a heart that keeps company with Allah, and it cannot be faked into a face that has gone dark within.
