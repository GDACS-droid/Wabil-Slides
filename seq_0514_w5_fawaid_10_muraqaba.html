It Leads to Watchfulness — and to Iḥsān

10
100

It bequeaths murāqaba, until it admits him into iḥsān

يُورِثُهُ الْمُرَاقَبَةَ حَتَّى يُدْخِلَهُ فِي بَابِ الْإِحْسَانِ. The fruit of constant remembrance is that the servant comes to feel himself watched — and so he worships Allah as though he sees Him. Ibn al-Qayyim then states the negative with a sharp image: there is no path for the one heedless of dhikr to reach the station of iḥsān — كَمَا لَا سَبِيلَ لِلْقَاعِدِ إِلَى الْوُصُولِ إِلَى الْبَيْتِ, “just as the one sitting still has no way of reaching the house.” You do not arrive at a place you are not walking toward. Dhikr is the walking; murāqaba is the road; iḥsān is the house at the end of it.

فَيَعْبُدُ اللَّهَ كَأَنَّهُ يَرَاهُ
Today

Iḥsān is not a mood that descends in the prayer; it is the residue of a day spent aware of Him. The one who has kept Allah in mind at the desk, in the car, in the quiet choice nobody saw, walks into ṣalāh already half-present — the watchfulness he is asked to summon there, he has been building all day. The heedless man, by contrast, tries to feel watched for the length of one rakʿah and cannot, because he has not been walking toward it.
