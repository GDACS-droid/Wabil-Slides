Tawbat al-Murāʾī

Does the Reward Return?

Two cases, two rulings

Ibn al-Qayyim takes up a question he says never left his mind, and on which he had not seen anyone give a satisfying answer: if a deed was spoiled by riyāʾ and the man then repents, does its reward return?

If he did the deed for other than Allah from the start, repentance does not turn it good again; the most it does is wipe away its punishment, so the deed stands neither for him nor against him.

But if he did it sincerely for Allah, and then conceit or showing-off crept in afterward, and then he repented and regretted — its reward may return to him and not be lost (some scholars held he must simply do the deed anew).

The principle he settled on

Good deeds and bad deeds push against one another, وَيَكُونُ الحُكْمُ لِلْغَالِبِ — the ruling goes to whichever overcomes, as the conqueror as good as erases the conquered. A sincere repentance, risen from صَمِيمِ القَلْبِ, burns away the sins it passed over until it is as if they never were — for the one who repents from a sin is like one who never sinned.
