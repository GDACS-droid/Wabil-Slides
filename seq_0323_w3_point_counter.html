Reading Him Faithfully

On Suspicion of the Muslims' Food

In the text

Ibn al-Qayyim's example of corrupt scruple is the ascetic who shunned the food of the Muslims for fear of doubtful provenance and ate instead from the lands of the disbelievers — “ruined,” he says, “by excessive ignorance, exaggerated extremism, thinking ill of the Muslims and well of the disbelievers.”

How we read it

We take his point fully: scruple that breeds bad opinion of the believers is a disease, not piety. We would only add, as our tradition stresses, that the cure is not to flatten all caution but to return to ḥusn al-ẓann with the Muslims and the default permissibility of their food. The straight path keeps both warmth toward the believers and a sober, non-obsessive care.
