Tamthīl al-Qulūb bi-l-Buyūt

Bring It Down Onto the Hearts

  • The empty, ruined house — the heart of the disbeliever and hypocrite, void of all good. It is shayṭān's own house, which he has secured and settled and made his dwelling. What is there to steal, when his stores, doubts, fantasies, and whisperings already fill it?
  • The king's house — the heart filled with the majesty of Allah, His love, watchfulness, and shyness before Him. What shayṭān would dare it? At most he snatches a momentary heedlessness — for the slave is human, and forgetfulness and lapse run over him.
  • The contested house — the heart that holds tawḥīd and love of Allah and the appetites of the nafs and the pulls of hawā. It sways between two callers: once the caller of faith and knowledge, once the caller of hawā and shayṭān. Here shayṭān has ambition, and bouts and battles.

And shayṭān can only master the contested heart through the weapons it already holds — appetites, doubts, fantasies, false hopes. He enters, finds his weapons lying there, takes them up and turns them on the heart. If the slave has a ready store of faith that outweighs them, he gets the better of shayṭān; if not, the day goes to the enemy. So when the slave opens his own door, lets the enemy in, and hands him the weapon to fight him with — he is the one to blame.
