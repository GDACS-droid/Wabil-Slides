A Useful Principle

When the Lesser Act Becomes the Better

Ibn al-Qayyim calls this “a most useful principle” — a fiqh of the self that distinguishes the excellence of a thing in itself from its excellence in its proper place:
  • In rukūʿ and sujūd, tasbīḥ outranks recitation — indeed reciting the Qurʾān there is forbidden; each act has its appointed word.
  • The fixed adhkār tied to their times — answering the muʾadhdhin, the dhikr after the prayer — outrank a general recitation in that moment.
  • If a fear of the shayāṭīn presses, the protective adhkār and supplications are better there than open recitation.
  • If an urgent need keeps the heart from presence in recitation, then turning to ask Allah for it — with a gathered heart — may be more beneficial right then.

“Give every possessor of a right his right; put everything in its place.” To busy oneself with the higher act out of its place, losing the lower act that the moment called for, is a loss the shayṭān profits from. Prayer ranks above each one alone, for it gathers recitation, dhikr and duʿāʾ together with the worship of all the limbs.
