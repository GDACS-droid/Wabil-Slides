The Gift He Will Not Accept

The image Ibn al-Qayyim presses

He turns the metaphor into a scene meant to shame us out of complacency: أَفَلَا يَسْتَحِي العَبْدُ أَنْ يُهْدِيَ إِلَى مَخْلُوقٍ مِثْلِهِ عَبْدًا مَيِّتًا؟ — “Is the servant not ashamed to gift, to a creature like himself, a dead slave?” Picture carrying a corpse, wrapped as a present, into the court of a king. What would he make of the offering, and of the one who dared bring it? Just so, he says, is the prayer emptied of خُشُوع and a heart gathered upon Allah: that dead gift, carried up to the King of kings. وَلِهَذَا لَا يَقْبَلُهَا اللَّهُ مِنْهُ وَلَا يُثِيبُهُ عَلَيْهَا — “for this Allah neither accepts it from him nor rewards him for it” — even where, in the rulings of this world, it discharges the obligation — “for the servant has of his prayer only what he was conscious of in it.”

Two ledgers, not one — and what a dead gift is

Hold the two halves of his ruling apart, because we blur them. In the فِقْه of validity, such a prayer can still discharge the فَرْض — you need not repeat it. But in the ledger of acceptance and reward, “He does not accept it.” A deed can be valid and unaccepted at once. The dead gift, then, is any deed done by the limbs with the heart wholly absent: رَكَعَات prayed on autopilot, Qurʾān recited by a tongue not listening to itself. A man who only asks “must I pray it again?” has answered the small question and missed Ibn al-Qayyim's: did any of it rise?
