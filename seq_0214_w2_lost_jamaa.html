Counting the Loss as You Would Count Money

The merchant's twenty-seven dīnārs

Ibn al-Qayyim illustrates that last sign — grieving a missed right — with a man who mourns missing the congregation, because he knows that even if his solitary prayer were accepted from him, فَقَدْ فَاتَهُ سَبْعَةٌ وَعِشْرُونَ ضِعْفًا — he has still lost twenty-seven times its reward. Then he holds it up against money to expose how dull our hearts are about the unseen: if a merchant — at home, with no travel and no hardship — lost twenty-seven dīnārs on a single transaction, لَأَكَلَ يَدَيْهِ نَدَمًا وَأَسَفًا, “he would bite his own hands in regret and grief” for days. And how can the two even be compared, Ibn al-Qayyim presses, when every single fold by which the جَمَاعَة multiplies the prayer is better than a thousand dīnārs, and a thousand thousand, and whatever Allah wills? So when the servant lets that profit slip and feels nothing, خَسِرَ قَطْعًا — “he has lost, for certain.”
The corollary

The diagnostic is exact: a man who misses a lucrative deal carries it heavily all evening, but misses جَمَاعَة and forgets it by the next breath. The gap between those two reactions is the precise measure of how thin his تَعْظِيم has worn — because he plainly believes the dīnārs are real and quietly suspects the reward is not.
