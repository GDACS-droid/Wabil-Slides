Nazaghāt al-Shayṭān

He Does Not Care Which Way You Fall

Allah commands nothing, says Ibn al-Qayyim, without the Shayṭān having two pulls on it: إِمَّا تَقْصِيرٌ وَتَفْرِيطٌ، وَإِمَّا إِفْرَاطٌ وَغُلُوٌّ — falling short, or going to excess. He does not care which of the two he wins; his one aim is to push the servant off the road, this one by never reaching it, that one by overshooting it.
  • If he finds you slack, he holds you back with laziness and false hopes — “there is time,” “Allah is forgiving” — until you abandon the command entirely.
  • If he finds you earnest and cannot slow you, he urges you past the mark: this is not enough; your aspiration is higher; they washed three times, so wash seven — until you overstep the straight path.
  • وَقَدْ فُتِنَ بِهَذَا أَكْثَرُ الخَلْقِ — most are tried by one of the two; nothing saves a servant but firm-rooted knowledge, faith, strength to fight him, and holding to the middle.
A corollary

Watch one worshipper across a single Ramaḍān. Riding zeal in the first nights, he piles voluntary fasts and night-prayers on himself until, by the second week, he is exhausted, short with his family, and skipping obligations he had managed easily before. So he swings the other way — “I overdid it” — and sinks to the bare minimum, then less. The enemy worked both ends of the same man, and got the abandonment he wanted by way of the excess.
