Foundation Two

Neither Slackened Nor Overstrained

Ibn al-Qayyim praises his teacher's definition — وَمَا أَحْسَنَ مَا قَالَ شَيْخُ الإِسْلَام — that to magnify the command and prohibition is that they be met three ways at once without:
  • a slack license بِتَرْخِيصٍ جَافٍّ — finding a dry, convenient reading that explains the order away and makes it easy on yourself.
  • an excessive severity بِتَشْدِيدٍ غَالٍ — piling harshness onto the order beyond what Allah actually asked, as though more burden were more piety.
  • a pretext that weakens the submission عَلَى عِلَّةٍ تُوهِنُ الانْقِيَاد — hanging the command on some rationale that loosens your obedience, so you follow the reason you invented rather than the One who commanded.
  • True reverence keeps the order exactly as it was given: not softened, not inflated, and not made to wait on an excuse.
The convenient license

A man hears that travel shortens the prayer, and now every short errand becomes “travel”; he hears that necessity lifts a prohibition, and his every craving becomes a “necessity.” The رُخْصَة is real and from Allah — but he is no longer reaching for it because Allah permitted it; he is reaching for it because it is comfortable, and bending the cause to fit. That is the تَرْخِيصٌ جَافّ Ibn Taymiyya warns against: the license used to dodge the command rather than to honor it.
