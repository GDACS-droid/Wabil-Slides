The Prayer

Summoned, and Looking Elsewhere

The man called before the sulṭān

Ibn al-Qayyim likens the one who turns in his prayer — with eye or heart — to a man summoned by the king, set to stand before him, while the king turns to him and speaks to him directly. And the man, through all of this, keeps glancing away right and left, or his heart has wandered off so that he does not even take in what is being said to him, because his heart is not present. What does such a man expect the king to do with him? At the very least he is dismissed from the king's presence in disgrace, fallen from his eyes.

“If you turned this way to a created king,” Ibn al-Qayyim presses, “what then of turning away from the Lord of the worlds, before whom you actually stand?” The slave whose heart is present — who feels the greatness of the One he stands before, whose neck is bowed and who is too ashamed before his Lord to look elsewhere — and the heedless slave are not equal: between their two prayers, said Ḥassān b. ʿAṭiyya, is as much as between the heavens and the earth.
