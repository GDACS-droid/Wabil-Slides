The Ādāb of Duʿāʾ · 4

Add to His Praise Your Own Poverty

The duʿāʾ preceded by praise is nearer to an answer than bare asking — and nearer still when the servant joins to it an honest report of his own state: his need, his poverty, his lowliness. Two pleas then meet in one supplication:

Two appeals that meet

  • From the Lord's side — His perfection, kindness and bounty: المُقْتَضِي مِنَ المَسْؤُول.
  • From the servant's side — his open confession of need, poverty and brokenness: المُقْتَضِي مِنَ السَّائِل.
  • When the two join, says Ibn al-Qayyim, the duʿāʾ is “more complete in knowledge and servanthood” — “such is the etiquette of servanthood.”

Three masters of the form

  • Mūsā: رَبِّ إِنِّي لِمَا أَنزَلْتَ إِلَيَّ مِنْ خَيْرٍ فَقِيرٌ (Q 28:24).
  • Dhū-l-Nūn: سُبْحَانَكَ إِنِّي كُنتُ مِنَ الظَّالِمِينَ (Q 21:87).
  • Ādam: رَبَّنَا ظَلَمْنَا أَنفُسَنَا (Q 7:23).
Today

You feel it already in how people ask one another. The applicant who opens by honestly praising what an employer has built, then plainly states his own need, moves the heart far more than the one who begins “give me the job.” “And Allah's is the highest likeness” (وَلِلَّهِ المَثَلُ الأَعْلَى): praise your Lord by His bounty, lay bare your poverty, and only then ask — it is nearer to having the need met.
