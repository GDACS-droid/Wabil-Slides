Tamthīl al-Qulūb bi-l-Buyūt

Bring It Down Onto the Hearts

  • The empty, ruined house — the heart of the disbeliever and hypocrite, void of all good. It is shayṭān's own house, which he has secured and settled. What is there to steal, when his stores and whisperings already fill it?
  • The king's house — the heart filled with the majesty of Allah, His love, watchfulness, and shyness before Him. What shayṭān would dare it? At most he snatches a momentary heedlessness — for the slave is human, and forgetfulness runs over him.
  • The contested house — the heart that holds tawḥīd and love of Allah, and also the appetites of the nafs and the pulls of hawā. It sways between two callers: once the caller of faith, once the caller of hawā and shayṭān. Here shayṭān has ambition, and bouts and battles.

Shayṭān can only master the contested heart through the weapons it already holds: appetites, doubts, fantasies, false hopes. He enters, finds his weapons there, and turns them on the heart. If the slave has ready against them a store of faith that outweighs them, he gets the better of shayṭān; if not, the day goes to the enemy.

So when the slave opens his own door and hands over the weapon — he is the one to blame.
