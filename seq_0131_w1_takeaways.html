Takeaways

What to Carry Out the Door

  • This is a letter on the heart and its ذِكْر by a Damascene master of تَزْكِيَة — student of Ibn Taymiyya, his companion even in prison — and we receive its تَزْكِيَة as the أُمَّة's shared inheritance, read with the تَحْقِيق.
  • The servant's whole life is three states: شُكْر (heart, tongue, and limbs), صَبْر (three restraints), and اِسْتِغْفَار — and his rank is set by his عُبُودِيَّة in what he hates, not only in what he loves.
  • A deed is weighed by the heart in it, not by its outward shape: a sin met with brokenness can lift you; a good deed met with عُجْب can sink you. The thing to fear is being left to your own self (خِذْلَان).
  • An upright heart rests on two pillars — loving Allah above every other love, and magnifying His command and prohibition — and you come in upon Allah by the nearest door there is: إِفْلَاس, pure need, never the claim of having arrived.

This week's وِرْد

Take on Sayyid al-Istighfār once each morning and once each evening — slowly, with its meaning, pausing at its two wings: أَبُوءُ لَكَ بِنِعْمَتِكَ عَلَيَّ (the favor) and وَأَبُوءُ بِذَنْبِي (the fault). Let it be the first thread of the daily وِرْد we build over the coming weeks.
