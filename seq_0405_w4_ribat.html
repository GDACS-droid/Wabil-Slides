The Struggle

The Watch Is Brief — Stand It

“Fight your enemy — the affair is near”

Ibn al-Qayyim does not leave the slave a captive. He pictures the command given to him: Fight your enemy and strive against him. Here are armies — take what you wish of them; here is armour — wear what you wish; here are fortresses — shelter in any you wish. Stand guard at the frontier until death, for the affair is near and the term of the watch is very short. It is as though the Great King has already sent His messengers, moved you to His own abode, set you free in the House of Honour, and locked your enemy in the harshest of prisons while you look on — the very prison he meant for you. All this as the reward of your patience in that brief span; and the hardship was but an hour, then it passed, as if it had never been.
A corollary

A soldier posted to a frontier for a fixed short tour does not unpack as if he will live there forever, nor does he desert on the hard nights; he holds the line because he knows the relief is dated and near. Ibn al-Qayyim is asking the believer to carry himself the same way through this life: the struggle against the nafs and the appetites is real and sometimes exhausting, but it is a posting, not a permanent home — “the term of the watch is very short.” The practical effect is not to romanticize hardship but to refuse two errors: settling into the dunyā as though the watch will never end, and abandoning the post because a particular night is long.
