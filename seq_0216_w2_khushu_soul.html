The Fruit

A Prayer Without a Soul In It

There is a right of the prayer that can be lost even while you are standing in it, and it is no side detail; it is the center: الخُشُوعُ فِي الصَّلَاةِ وَحُضُورُ القَلْبِ فِيهَا بَيْنَ يَدَيِ الرَّبّ, “khushūʿ in the prayer and the presence of the heart before the Lord,” الَّذِي هُوَ رُوحُهَا وَلُبُّهَا — “which is its very soul and its kernel.”

A body with no spirit in it

فَصَلَاةٌ بِلَا خُشُوعٍ وَلَا حُضُورٍ كَبَدَنٍ مَيِّتٍ لَا رُوحَ فِيه — “a prayer with no khushūʿ and no presence is like a dead body with no soul in it.” The image is a corpse, not merely a sick body: the form can be perfectly intact — the rukūʿ correct, the sujūd touching the ground, the words recited in order — while the thing that made it a prayer has left. This is why khushūʿ falls under taʿẓīm: to stand before the Lord and let the heart roam the marketplace is to fail to magnify the very One you are standing before.

Where the soul leaks out

The soul of the prayer leaks out at the seams: the mind on the day's worry through al-Fātiḥah, back for a moment at rukūʿ, gone again through the tashahhud, until you reach the salām unsure what you recited. Ibn al-Qayyim's question is not the jurist's “does it count?” but “is there a soul in it?” Khushūʿ is simply the discipline of bringing the wandering heart back, again and again, to the One addressed — and the prayer is alive in exact proportion to how often you bring it back.
