The Muqaddima

The Nearest Door, and the Thickest Veil

Entering by the door of pure need

From the two wings he draws his sharpest line: أَقْرَبُ بَابٍ دَخَلَ مِنْهُ العَبْدُ عَلَى اللهِ بَابُ الإِفْلَاسِ — “the nearest door by which a servant comes in upon Allah is the door of bankruptcy.” He sees in himself no standing, no station, no deed he can lean on or favor he can hold over his Lord; he comes in by sheer اِفْتِقَار — naked need — “the way one enters whose heart poverty and lowliness have so broken that the crack has reached its core (سُوَيْدَاءَهُ) and split it,” seeing that in every atom of him, outward and inward, is a complete need of his Lord, and that were He to leave him for the blink of an eye he would be destroyed. And against this door he sets the warning that closes the section: لَا طَرِيقَ إِلَى اللهِ أَقْرَبَ مِنَ العُبُودِيَّةِ، وَلَا حِجَابَ أَغْلَظَ مِنَ الدَّعْوَى — “no road to Allah is nearer than servanthood, and no veil is thicker than الدَّعْوَى — the claim” — the soul's pretension that it is something, that it has arrived.
In practice

Two people pray for the same thing — a sick child healed, a marriage mended. One comes leaning on his record: I have prayed my whole life, I fast, I give — surely this is owed to me. That leaning is the دَعْوَى, the claim, the thickest veil; even his worship has become a thing he holds over his Lord. The other comes empty-handed: I have nothing to bring You, no deed I trust, only my need of You — and You are the only One who can. That second one has come in by the door of إِفْلَاس, and Ibn al-Qayyim says it is the nearest door there is. The strange mercy of being broken by a hardship is that it can knock the claim out of a person's hands and walk him to that door.
