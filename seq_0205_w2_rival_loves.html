Foundation One

What We Quietly Put Ahead of Him

Ibn al-Qayyim is blunt about how often we fail this, and how respectable the rivals look: وَمَا أَكْثَرَ مَا يُقَدِّمُ العَبْدُ مَا يُحِبُّهُ هُوَ وَيَهْوَاهُ.
  • His own desire — what the servant himself loves and craves, his hawā: the path of least resistance dressed up as a reasonable preference.
  • The love of a man who has power over him — what is loved by his كَبِير, his أَمِير, or his shaykh: pleasing someone whose approval he needs, even when it crosses what Allah loves.
  • The love of his own family — placing the pleasure of those closest to him above the pleasure of Allah, because their displeasure is felt at home and His is not felt at all.
  • In any of these, Ibn al-Qayyim rules, love of Allah has not truly come ahead of every beloved; وَلَا كَانَتْ هِيَ الحَاكِمَةَ المُؤْمِرَة — “nor is it the love that rules and commands the rest.” It has been quietly demoted from sovereign to one vote among many.
A lawful love can do it too

The danger is not only sin. A man can love a halal marriage, a respected teacher, or his own good name — all permitted — and still let one of them sit, unexamined, above Allah: he will skip the jamāʿah rather than be seen leaving a gathering early, or soften a clear ruling so his family stays pleased. The love was lawful; its rank was not.
