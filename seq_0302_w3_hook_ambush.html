Madākhil al-Shayṭān

He Comes Only by Ambush

Ibn al-Qayyim opens with a precise claim: the enemy cannot break into the believer head-on. The Qurʾān denies Iblīs any real sulṭān over the servants of Allah — they are in His keeping, under His shade — so when the enemy lands a blow, it is never by overpowering the believer. He gets at him the way a thief gets at a sleeping man: غِيلَةً عَلَى غِرَّةٍ وَغَفْلَةٍ — by stealth, in a moment of carelessness.
  • Ghafla — heedlessness, the unguarded moment when Allah is forgotten. This is the master-door; the other two only open through it.
  • Shahwa — appetite. He dresses a forbidden desire in sweetness and presses you toward it.
  • Ghaḍab — anger. He inflames you until you wrong yourself or another.
  • Guard yourself as you will, Ibn al-Qayyim says, you remain قَدْ بُلِيَ بِالغَفْلَةِ وَالشَّهْوَةِ وَالغَضَبِ — tried with all three. The aim is watchfulness at the door, not a fantasy of immunity.

Even Ādam was reached this way

Ādam was the most knowing and most steadfast of creation, and still the enemy did not relent until he made him slip. “So what of the moth of forbearance,” Ibn al-Qayyim asks, “whose mind beside his father's is a drop in the sea?” The conclusion is sober vigilance and humility — never despair.
