Tamthīl al-Qulūb bi-l-Buyūt

Which House Will the Thief Rob?

Three houses, one thief

A house belonging to the king, holding his treasures, stores, and jewels. A house belonging to a subject, holding the subject's own goods, with none of the king's in it. And an empty house with nothing in it at all.

A thief comes to rob one of the three — which will he choose? Not the empty house: there is nothing in it to take. (Hence Ibn ʿAbbās رضي الله عنهما, told that the Jews claim they get no whisperings in their prayer, replied: “What would شَيْطَان do with a ruined heart?”) Not the king's house: it has guards and soldiers around it the thief cannot even approach — and its guard is the King Himself; how could a thief draw near it? There remains only the third house — the subject's — and that is the one on which he launches his raid.
A corollary

This overturns a comfortable assumption. We tend to read an undisturbed prayer — no whispers, no inner struggle — as a sign of strength. The parable warns that it can be the opposite: the thief leaves the ruined house alone precisely because it has nothing left worth stealing. So if a person feels no resistance at all when reaching for what Allah forbade, no tug of conscience, no battle in his صَلَاة, the honest first question is not “how peaceful I am” but “is there anything of إِيمَان left in the house for the enemy to bother with?” The believer's struggle against the whisper, by contrast, is itself evidence that his heart still holds the King's treasure.
