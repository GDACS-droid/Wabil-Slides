The Muqaddima

How You Can Tell a Heart That Magnifies

  • He performs the command for Allah, not for the eyes of people — and avoids the forbidden out of awe of Allah, not merely fear of the legal penalty or of falling in others' esteem.
  • He guards the command's times, its limits, its pillars and its perfections — racing to it when it falls due, careful to do it well, not merely to discharge it.
  • He feels real grief, gloom and regret when any right of it slips — as a man grieves to miss the congregation, knowing he has lost the twenty-seven-fold reward of praying with it.
  • He minds what the soul shrugs off: the first of the time (the pleasure of Allah), the first row (upon whose right Allah and His angels send blessings), the larger gathering, and above all the khushūʿ that is the prayer's very spirit.
A corollary

His own startling test: if a merchant lost twenty-seven dīnārs on a single deal, in his own town, with no travel and no toil, he would “bite his hands in grief.” Yet a man misses the congregation — whose every multiplied unit is worth more than a thousand dīnārs, and a thousand thousand — and feels nothing. “A cold heart, untroubled by the loss” — that numbness, says Ibn al-Qayyim, is itself the proof that the command is not magnified in it.
