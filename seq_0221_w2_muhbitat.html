The Principle Applied

The Nullifiers Outnumber Counting

If expiation hangs on the absence of nullifiers, Ibn al-Qayyim names them, warning that مُحْبِطَاتُ الأَعْمَالِ وَمُفْسِدَاتُهَا أَكْثَرُ مِنْ أَنْ تُحْصَر, “what voids and spoils deeds is more than can be counted”:
  • رِيَاء — “though it be subtle, it spoils the deed,” and it is “many doors beyond counting.”
  • عُجْب, self-admiration in the deed; and المَنُّ عَلَى اللَّه, inwardly reminding Allah of one's act as though doing Him a favor.
  • Seeking people's praise by it — befriending whoever exalts you for it, resenting whoever does not, feeling “cheated of your due.”
  • A deed untied from the سُنَّة غَيْرُ مُقَيَّدٍ بِاتِّبَاعِ السُّنَّة — its disconnection from the Prophet's ﷺ way renders it void.
  • The مَنّ and أَذَى that cancel charity — wounding the one you gave to, or parading the favor.
يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا لَا تُبْطِلُوا صَدَقَاتِكُم بِالْمَنِّ وَالْأَذَىٰ
“O you who believe, do not nullify your charities with reminders of your generosity and with injury.”
Sūrat al-Baqarah 2:264
