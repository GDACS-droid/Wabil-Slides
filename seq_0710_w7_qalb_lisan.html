The Three Degrees

Heart and Tongue Together, Then the Heart Alone

  • Highest: dhikr upon which the heart and the tongue agree (مَا تَوَاطَأَ عَلَيْهِ القَلْبُ وَاللِّسَان).
  • Second: dhikr of the heart alone.
  • Third: dhikr of the tongue alone.

Why does the heart's dhikr outweigh the tongue's? Because, he says, “the dhikr of the heart yields knowledge (يُثْمِرُ المَعْرِفَة), stirs love, sows shame, awakens fear, calls to vigilance, and restrains from falling short in obedience and from making light of sins.” The tongue's dhikr alone yields none of this — “and if it bears any of it, its fruit is weak.”

A corollary

So the cure for a dry tongue is not to stop saying the words — it is to make the heart catch up to them. Say سُبْحَانَ اللَّه and pause long enough to mean it; let one understood phrase weigh more than a hundred run off by a wandering mind. The goal is never to abandon the tongue, but to bring the heart with it until the two agree.
