Fawāʾid al-Dhikr · 46–47

It Melts the Heart's Hardness and Cures Its Sickness

Fawāʾid al-dhikr
47
73

The heart is sick; its medicine is ذِكْر

First the hardness (46): there is in the heart a قَسْوَة that nothing melts but the remembrance of Allah. A man complained to al-Ḥasan of his hard heart; he answered in two words: أَذِبْهُ بِالذِّكْرِ — “melt it with remembrance” — for the deeper the heedlessness, the harder the heart, and when it remembers, that hardness runs off “as lead melts in the fire.” Then the diagnosis beneath it (47): hearts are not merely hard, they are diseased — الذِّكْرُ شِفَاءُ القَلْبِ وَدَوَاؤُهُ، وَالغَفْلَةُ مَرَضُهُ — and their cure and their medicine both lie in remembering Allah.

أَنَّ الذِّكْرَ شِفَاءُ القَلْبِ وَدَوَاؤُهُ، وَالغَفْلَةَ مَرَضُهُ
In practice

Listen to the dose in the poet's complaint Ibn al-Qayyim cites: إِذَا مَرِضْنَا تَدَاوَيْنَا بِذِكْرِكُمْ / فَنَتْرُكُ الذِّكْرَ أَحْيَانًا فَنَنْتَكِسُ — “When we sickened we were healed by remembering you; then we leave the remembrance awhile, and we relapse.” A medicine that works only while taken is still working — the relapse is not its failure but its proof. Do not measure ذِكْر by the calm of one good sitting; measure it like a daily prescription, missed at the cost of a relapse you will feel by week's end.
