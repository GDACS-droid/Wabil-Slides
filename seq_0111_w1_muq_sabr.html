The Muqaddima

Patience Is Three Restraints

وَالصَّبْرُ: حَبْسُ النَّفْسِ عَنِ التَّسَخُّطِ بِالمَقْدُورِ، وَحَبْسُ اللِّسَانِ عَنِ الشَّكْوَى، وَحَبْسُ الجَوَارِحِ عَنِ المَعْصِيَةِ
“Patience is: restraining the soul from raging at the decree, restraining the tongue from complaint, and restraining the limbs from disobedience.”
wa-ṣ-ṣabru: ḥabsu n-nafsi ʿani t-tasakhkhuṭi bi-l-maqdūr, wa-ḥabsu l-lisāni ʿani sh-shakwā, wa-ḥabsu l-jawāriḥi ʿani l-maʿṣiya
Ibn al-Qayyim, al-Wābil al-Ṣayyib

What it forbids, and what it does not

He names the breaches by their old faces: اللَّطْم (striking the cheeks), شَقُّ الثِّيَاب (tearing the garment), نَتْفُ الشَّعْر (pulling the hair). Ṣabr holds soul, tongue and limb together. But mark the precision: it forbids raging at the decree, not the tears themselves, and not turning to Allah in complaint. Yaʿqūb said إِنَّمَا أَشْكُو بَثِّي وَحُزْنِي إِلَى اللَّهِ (Yūsuf 12:86), “I only complain of my anguish and grief to Allah.” When the servant keeps all three, he says, انْقَلَبَتِ المِحْنَةُ فِي حَقِّهِ مِنْحَةً — the affliction is turned, for him, into a gift.
