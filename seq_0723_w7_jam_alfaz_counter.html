Reading the Text Soundly

On Joining the Different Worded Reports

In the text

On the duʿāʾ of Abū Bakr — narrated both with كَثِيرًا (“much”) and كَبِيرًا (“great”) — al-Nawawī (may Allah have mercy on him) held it good to combine the two wordings and say both. Ibn al-Qayyim, following his own school's masters, argues against combining, calling it “an innovated way no known imām preceded,” and prefers saying one wording at a time.

How we read it

We engage the principle, not the persons. A sound and gentle rule: where the Prophet ﷺ is reliably reported to have said a word, saying it is sunna; where the narrators differed over a single word, there is room and no harm either way — to choose one, or, as our own tradition has held, to join two synonymous variants in seeking to capture the certain wording. The disagreement is one of awlā (what is preferable), not ḥalāl and ḥarām; we keep the adab of the supplication and let the heart's presence, not the count of words, be the measure.
