Fawāʾid al-Dhikr · 39–41

It Gathers the Scattered, Wakes the Sleeper, Fruits the Stations

  • 39 — It gathers what was scattered and scatters what should not gather. ذِكْر draws back together the heart, will and resolve that the world had pulled into pieces — “all torment lies in their being scattered, and all life and bliss in the heart's gathering.” And it scatters the armies it should: the heaped-up worries and the regrets over lost shares, the piled sins that drop away and dissolve, and the relays of Iblīs — for he keeps sending raiding-party after raiding-party, and the stronger a man's pull toward Allah the heavier and more fierce the raid; nothing breaks up that siege but constant ذِكْر.
  • 40 — It wakes the heart from its sleep. The heedless heart is asleep, its trade running at a loss; when ذِكْر wakes it and it sees what slipped past in the sleep, “it girds its waist and revives what is left of its life” — for الغَفْلَةُ نَوْمٌ ثَقِيلٌ, heedlessness is a heavy sleep.
  • 41 — It is the tree whose fruit is every station. ذِكْر is a tree that fruits the realizations and states the travellers seek; from wakefulness up to تَوْحِيد, every مَقَام grows on it as its root — “and the greater the tree and the deeper its root, the greater its fruit.”
A corollary

So a man does not first acquire patience, then gratitude, then love, then add ذِكْر as a fourth. The order runs the other way: he keeps the tree of ذِكْر watered, and those stations fruit on their own. If your inner life feels barren of states you read about, Ibn al-Qayyim's diagnosis is not “you lack patience” — it is “you have let the one tree they all grow on go dry.”
