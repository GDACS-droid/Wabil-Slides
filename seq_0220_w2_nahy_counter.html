Reading Him Carefully

When a Deed Is Cancelled

يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا لَا تُبْطِلُوا صَدَقَاتِكُم بِالْمَنِّ وَالْأَذَىٰ
“O you who believe, do not nullify your charities with reminders of your generosity and with injury.”
Sūrat al-Baqarah 2:264
In the text

Citing al-Ḥujurāt 49:2 — the warning that raising voices above the Prophet's ﷺ can make deeds collapse أَن تَحْبَطَ أَعْمَالُكُمْ — Ibn al-Qayyim notes that this is “not apostasy, but a sin by which the deed is nullified,” reading the verb as a real cancelling of works by major sins.

How we read it

We affirm with him that riyāʾ and reminders of one's favor spoil the deed they touch. On whether other major sins literally annul all prior good, our tradition holds — following the Ashʿarī reading of إِحْبَاط — that only disbelief erases the whole record; a believer's sins diminish and endanger reward, but do not wipe out faith and its fruits. Take his warning to heart, and keep the hope the verses of mercy keep open.
