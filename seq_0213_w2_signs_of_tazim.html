ʿAlāmāt al-Taʿẓīm

What Real Reverence Looks Like

If the inward reverence is invisible, how is it known? Ibn al-Qayyim gives its marks — فَعَلَامَةُ التَّعْظِيمِ لِلْأَوَامِر — and they are concrete, all about how the deed is handled:
  • Guarding their times and limits رِعَايَةُ أَوْقَاتِهَا وَحُدُودِهَا — keeping the appointment exactly, not letting it slide to the edge of its window.
  • Inspecting their pillars, obligations, and completeness التَّفْتِيشُ عَلَى أَرْكَانِهَا وَوَاجِبَاتِهَا وَكَمَالِهَا — auditing the arkān and wājibāt, not merely scraping past the minimum.
  • Eagerness to do them beautifully and on time وَالحِرْصُ عَلَى تَحْسِينِهَا وَفِعْلِهَا فِي أَوْقَاتِهَا, and hurrying to them the moment they fall due المُسَارَعَةُ إِلَيْهَا عِنْدَ وُجُوبِهَا.
  • Grief, dejection, and sorrow when any of their rights is lost الحُزْنُ وَالكَآبَةُ وَالأَسَفُ عِنْدَ فَوَاتِ حَقٍّ مِنْ حُقُوقِهَا — the heart does not shrug; it actually mourns.
Today

These signs translate into questions you can ask yourself before sleep: did I pray at the start of the time or only catch it before it expired? Did I stand for it fully, or rush through it while the mind was elsewhere? And the truest gauge — the last sign — when I prayed badly or nearly lost a ṣalāh, did it bother me at all, or did the moment pass without a ripple? A heart that magnifies the command grieves a thin prayer the way you would grieve a real loss.
