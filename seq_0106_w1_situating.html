How We Read Him

Faithful, and Contextualized

In the text

al-Wābil al-Ṣayyib is a Ḥanbalī work by a student of Ibn Taymiyya. On a few questions — certain creedal asides, an occasional remark on “the people of kalām” — its author writes from his own school, which on those points reads differently from the Ashʿarī way.

How we read it

The heart of this book — the living heart, the fruits of dhikr, the prophetic adhkār — is the shared inheritance of the whole umma, and we receive it warmly. Where a point genuinely diverges we will name the view, in one calm sentence, and move on. We engage the idea, never the man; and we do not go hunting for conflict where the text gives us none — which, in this book, is most of the time.

Read with the taḥqīq, not the rumor

One concrete habit this demands tonight: when the letter says قَالَ شَيْخُ الإِسْلَام, the editor's apparatus shows it can mean not Ibn Taymiyya but al-Harawī al-Anṣārī, author of Manāzil al-Sāʾirīn. A later copyist guessed wrong and “corrected” the name. So we cite from the verified edition — not from what a margin assumes.
