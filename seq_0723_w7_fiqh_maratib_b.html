A Most Useful Principle

The Dusty Robe and the Filthy Robe

Why is “which act is better?” sometimes the wrong question? Because the answer depends on the state of the one asking — and Ibn al-Qayyim records an image from his shaykh that settles it exactly:
A corollary

Imām Ibn Taymiyya's image, which Ibn al-Qayyim records: a robe that is merely dusty is best cleaned with perfume and rose-water, but a robe that is filthy needs soap and hot water. Asked which is better for the servant, tasbīḥ or istighfār, he answered, “And how, when the robes are never free of filth?” Give every right its right; put each thing in its place — and let the prayer, which gathers recitation, dhikr and duʿāʾ with the worship of all the limbs, rank above each of them alone.

The rule the image teaches

Ibn al-Qayyim turns the picture into a precise rule: فَيُعْطَى كُلُّ ذِي حَقٍّ حَقَّهُ، وَيُوضَعُ كُلُّ شَيْءٍ مَوْضِعَهُ — every right is given its right, and each thing is set in its place. Just as each limb has its appointed task (“the eye has its place, the foot its place”), so each act of worship has a moment that is its moment. He calls keeping these ranks مِنْ تَمَامِ الحِكْمَةِ — part of the very perfection of wisdom. So the mark of a discerning worshipper is not that he reaches for the highest-ranked act at every moment, but that he reads his own state — dusty or filthy, fearful, heedless, or in urgent need — and answers it with the act that state calls for.
