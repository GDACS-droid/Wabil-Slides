Athar al-Dhanb

A Sin That Becomes a Door

When Allah wills good for a servant after he falls, He opens a door of التَّوْبَةِ وَالنَّدَمِ وَالاِنْكِسَارِ وَالذُّلِّ وَالاِفْتِقَارِ — repentance, regret, brokenness, lowliness, and need — until the very sin becomes a cause of mercy, and the enemy says: “Would that I had left him and never made him fall.”

How a sin can outweigh many obediences

A servant sins, and the sin stays before his eyes — afraid of it, grieving, weeping, ashamed before his Lord, head bowed, heart broken. That brokenness, Ibn al-Qayyim says, can be more beneficial to him than many acts of worship, until the sin becomes the cause of his happiness. The danger was never the falling alone; it is falling and then standing tall in self-satisfaction.

A corollary

Watch the deed after it is done. One man replays a careless slip and stays soft and watchful; another replays a kindness and warms to himself each time he tells it. The sin that leaves you bowed may serve you better than the virtue that leaves you pleased with yourself.
