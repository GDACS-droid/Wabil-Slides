Fawāʾid al-Dhikr

It Pleases Him, Lifts the Grief, Brings the Joy

  • 2 — It pleases al-Raḥmān. يُرْضِي الرَّحْمَنَ. Before ذِكْر is a balm for you, it is beloved to Him; the tongue moving with His name itself earns His good pleasure. So the smallest remembrance already has its worth, before any benefit returns to you.
  • 3 — It removes anxiety and grief from the heart. يُزِيلُ الْهَمَّ وَالْغَمَّ عَنِ الْقَلْبِ. The clouded heart — heavy with هَمّ (dread of what is coming) and غَمّ (grief over what has passed) — finds that weight lift under His remembrance. Ibn al-Qayyim’s reason is exact: the heart was created needing its Lord, and rests in nothing else; turned away from Him it constricts, and turned to Him it opens.
  • 4 — It brings the heart joy, gladness, and expansion. الْفَرَحَ وَالسُّرُورَ وَالْبَسْطَ. Beyond merely lifting grief, ذِكْر fills the space with something: the heedless heart is constricted (مَقْبُوض); the heart in ذِكْر is expanded (مَبْسُوط) with the gladness of a heart that has found its Lord.

The reward that is already here

Ibn al-Qayyim presses this further in the book: the anguish, anxiety, and grief of the one who turns away are not only punishments stored up for later; they are عُقُوبَاتٌ عَاجِلَةٌ، وَنَارٌ دُنْيَوِيَّةٌ, an immediate punishment, a worldly fire, a present Hell. And the opening, ease, and gladness of the one who remembers is a paradise hastened — a Garden in this world before the Garden of the next.
