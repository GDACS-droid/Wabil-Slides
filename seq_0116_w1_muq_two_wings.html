The ʿĀrif Flies on Two Wings

How the servant travels to Allah

When Allah wills good for a servant, Ibn al-Qayyim says, He opens for him a door of turning back — of regret, of brokenness, of need and pleading. The servant then walks to Allah between two perceptions: seeing Allah's favor upon him, which gives birth to love, thankfulness and praise; and seeing the faults of his own self and deeds, which gives birth to humility and a sense of his own bankruptcy. He cites the words العَارِفُ يَسِيرُ إِلَى اللهِ بَيْنَ مُشَاهَدَةِ المِنَّةِ وَمُطَالَعَةِ عَيْبِ النَّفْسِ وَالعَمَلِ — “the knower travels to Allah between witnessing the favor and inspecting the fault of self and deed” — words the editor traces to al-Harawī al-Anṣārī in Manāzil al-Sāʾirīn. Lose either wing, he warns, and the servant is like a bird that has lost one of its two wings.
