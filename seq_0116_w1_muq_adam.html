The Muqaddima

If Ādam, Then What of Us?

The argument from the father of mankind

Ibn al-Qayyim presses the point with a single, humbling comparison. Ādam — أَبُو البَشَر, the father of mankind — was أَعْلَمَ الخَلْقِ وَأَرْجَحَهُمْ عَقْلًا وَأَثْبَتَهُمْ: the most knowing of creation, the soundest in intellect, the firmest in resolve. And still the enemy did not leave him until he led him into what he led him into. “So what,” the author asks, “of the one whose intellect beside his father's is like a drop of spittle in the sea?” The lesson is not despair but realism: if the strongest mind that ever was could be reached through a moment of غَفْلَة, then no servant should imagine himself sealed shut. لَا بُدَّ لَهُ مِنْ غَفْلَةٍ، وَلَا بُدَّ لَهُ مِنْ شَهْوَةٍ، وَلَا بُدَّ لَهُ مِنْ غَضَبٍ — every one of us has these three openings, guard as we may. The question the book then answers is not whether you will slip, but what you do in the hour after you do.
A corollary

So a person who has guarded his prayer for years should not be scandalized when an old appetite, a flash of anger, or an unguarded afternoon catches him out. More dangerously, he should not let that shock harden into “someone like me does not do this” and then into despair. Ibn al-Qayyim's point cuts the legs from under both reactions: the slip was always possible — Ādam himself was reached — so it is neither proof that your years of obedience were a lie, nor permission to give up. It is only the call to the door of return the next slide opens.
