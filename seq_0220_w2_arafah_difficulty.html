The Principle Applied

Resolving an Old Difficulty

صَوْمُ يَوْمِ عَرَفَةَ يُكَفِّرُ سَنَتَيْن، وَيَوْمُ عَاشُورَاءَ يُكَفِّرُ سَنَة
“Fasting the Day of ʿArafah expiates two years, and the Day of ʿĀshūrāʾ expiates a year.”
Recorded by Muslim 1162, from Abū Qatāda

The puzzle, and the answer from the two foundations

The objection: if a man habitually fasts ʿArafah and ʿĀshūrāʾ every year, how is three years' worth of sins wiped out each year — what is left to expiate? Ibn al-Qayyim answers with the principle just laid down. Expiation is مَشْرُوطٌ بِشُرُوط، مَوْقُوفٌ عَلَى انْتِفَاءِ مَوَانِع — “conditioned by conditions, suspended upon the absence of nullifiers,” in the deed and around it. A fast that meets every condition with the heart present does expiate fully. But عَمَلٌ شَمِلَتْهُ الغَفْلَة، وَفَقَدَ الإِخْلَاصَ الَّذِي هُوَ رُوحُهُ وَلُبُّه — a deed swallowed by heedlessness, void of the sincerity that is its soul — “what does that expiate?” The reckoning was never in the bare act, but in guarding the act from what spoils it.
