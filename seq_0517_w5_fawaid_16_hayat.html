It Gives Life to the Heart

It bequeaths the very life of the heart

This is the heart of the book. Ibn al-Qayyim writes: “I heard Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymiyya — may Allah sanctify his soul — say: dhikr is to the heart as water is to the fish; so what is the state of the fish when it is parted from the water?” Mark the comparison: the fish does not merely enjoy water, nor merely improve in it — it lives by it, and the instant it is parted from it, it gasps and dies. So too the heart and dhikr. This is why the ḥadīth of the living and the dead frames the whole work: heedlessness is no small lapse or missed reward; it is the heart out of the only element in which it can stay alive.

الذِّكْرُ لِلْقَلْبِ مِثْلُ الْمَاءِ لِلسَّمَكِ
A corollary

If dhikr is water, then a long stretch of heedlessness is not “a dry spell” to shrug at — it is a fish left on the shore. That is why the heart, after weeks away from His remembrance, feels strangely flat: not sinful exactly, just airless. The cure is not a grand resolution but to put the fish back in the water — to start the tongue moving again, today, even with one word.
