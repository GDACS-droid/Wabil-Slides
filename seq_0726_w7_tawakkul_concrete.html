The Balance Made Concrete

Tie the Camel — and Trust

المُؤْمِنُ القَوِيُّ خَيْرٌ وَأَحَبُّ إِلَى اللَّهِ مِنَ المُؤْمِنِ الضَّعِيفِ، وَفِي كُلٍّ خَيْرٌ
“The strong believer is better and more beloved to Allah than the weak believer — though in each there is good.”
From the same ḥadīth of Abū Hurayra — recorded by Muslim

Why does the Prophet ﷺ open the hadith of reliance by praising strength and ending it with “do not be weak”? Because the means are not a failure of trust; they are part of it. Allah, who decreed the outcome, decreed the medicine and the effort and the precaution as the road to it. The Bedouin asked, “Shall I tie my camel and trust, or leave it loose and trust?” and the answer was: tie it, then trust.

In practice

So you take the medicine the doctor prescribed and place the cure with Allah; you study fully for the exam and ask Him to open your mind; you lock the door and pay the zakāt and seek the job and say “ḥasbiya llāh.” The one who skips the means and calls it tawakkul has not relied on Allah; he has disobeyed Him, for Allah commanded the means. al-taslīm comes after badhl al-juhd, never instead of it.
