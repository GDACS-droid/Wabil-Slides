Reading Him Carefully

Not the Doing, but the Guarding

His own conclusion to the whole passage

He gathers it into one line that should reorder how we think about worship: وَلَيْسَ الشَّأْنُ فِي العَمَل، إِنَّمَا الشَّأْنُ فِي حِفْظِ العَمَلِ مِمَّا يُفْسِدُهُ وَيُحْبِطُه — “the matter is not in doing the deed; it is in guarding the deed from what corrupts and voids it.” The decisive work begins after the deed is done: most exhaust themselves piling up acts yet never protect them from riyāʾ, ʿujb, and mann.

In the text

Citing al-Ḥujurāt 49:2 — the warning that raising voices above the Prophet's ﷺ can make deeds collapse أَن تَحْبَطَ أَعْمَالُكُمْ — Ibn al-Qayyim notes this was “not apostasy, but a sin by which the deed is nullified,” reading the verb as a real cancelling of works by major sins.

How we read it

We affirm fully with him that riyāʾ, ʿujb, and mann spoil the very deed they touch — that is his point, and it is sound. On whether a major sin annuls all prior good, our tradition holds — on the Ashʿarī reading of إِحْبَاط — that only disbelief erases the whole record; a believer's sins diminish and endanger reward, but do not wipe out faith and its fruits. Take every warning to heart, and keep the hope the verses of mercy keep open.
