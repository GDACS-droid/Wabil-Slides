Who Was Ibn al-Qayyim?

شَمْس الدِّين مُحَمَّد بن أبي بَكْر بن أيُّوب بن سَعْد
known as Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya — “son of the قَيِّم (steward) of the Jawziyya school”
  • Born 691H (1292) in Damascus, into a learned household; his father was the قَيِّم (administrator) of a madrasa named for Ibn al-Jawzī. The family took its name from that office, not from a bloodline.
  • He came of age under the Mamlūks, in a Damascus that had become the great surviving hub of حَدِيث and فِقْه in the generation after the Mongol sack of Baghdad.
  • He studied broadly — حَدِيث, فِقْه, أُصُول, Arabic, and the laws of inheritance (فَرَائِض) from his own father — and taught, issued فَتْوَى, and wrote in that city until he died in 751H (1350).
  • His students record the man, not only the scholar: Ibn Kathīr, al-Dhahabī and Ibn Rajab describe him as constant in ذِكْر, long in تَهَجُّد, slow to speak ill of anyone — one who would lengthen his prayer and his prostration until those near him wondered at it.
