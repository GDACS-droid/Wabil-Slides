Taʿẓīm al-Amr

The Sign of an Honouring Heart

A man may perform the command and avoid the prohibition only for the eyes of people — to keep his standing among them, or out of fear of a worldly penalty. Ibn al-Qayyim says this doing and leaving is not issuing from تَعْظِيم of the command at all, nor from honouring the One who commanded.
  • The sign of honouring the commands: guarding their times and limits, examining their pillars, conditions, and completeness, hastening to them the moment they fall due, and grieving when any part of their due is lost.
  • His example: a man who grieves at missing the congregation — knowing that praying alone forfeits twenty-seven times the reward — the way a trader would bite his hands in regret over losing twenty-seven dīnārs in one deal, in his own town, with no travel or hardship. And every multiple of the congregation's reward, Ibn al-Qayyim notes, is better than a thousand, and a thousand thousand.
  • Beyond the congregation: grieving the lost first row, the lost beginning of the time, the lost خُشُوع. A man may pray with a cold heart, untouched by any of these losses — and the scholars say of him that he scarcely has a prayer, for a servant gets from his prayer only what he was present for.
