The Muqaddima

The عَارِف Flies on Two Wings

How the servant actually travels to Allah

When Allah wills good for a servant, He opens him a door of return — regret, brokenness, neediness, pleading. The servant then walks to Allah between two sights. The first: witnessing Allah's favor upon him (مُشَاهَدَةُ المِنَّة) — which gives birth to love, praise and thanks to the Bestower of blessings. The second: inspecting the faults of his own self and deeds (مُطَالَعَةُ عَيْبِ النَّفْسِ وَالعَمَل) — which gives birth to humility, broken-heartedness, and a sense that one stands before Allah مُفْلِسًا, bankrupt. The full image: العَارِفُ يَسِيرُ إِلَى اللهِ بَيْنَ مُشَاهَدَةِ المِنَّةِ وَمُطَالَعَةِ عَيْبِ النَّفْسِ وَالعَمَلِ — words the verified edition traces to al-Harawī al-Anṣārī in Manāzil al-Sāʾirīn. Lose either wing, he warns, and the servant is كَالطَّيْرِ الَّذِي فَقَدَ أَحَدَ جَنَاحَيْهِ — like a bird that has lost one of its two wings: favor alone breeds conceit, and fault alone breeds despair. He flies on both or not at all.
In practice

You can recognize a one-winged believer in yourself. The person who flies on the favor alone speaks of his good record — his Qurʾān, his charity, his years of practice — with a quiet satisfaction that has stopped noticing its own faults: that is the conceit Ibn al-Qayyim warns of. The person who flies on the fault alone can list every sin and shortcoming but cannot bring himself to feel that Allah has been generous to him; he prays, but under a low cloud, half-expecting to be turned away. The remedy is not to choose between them but to hold both in the same sitting: count one mercy Allah has shown you and one fault you owe Him repentance for — that is the bird beating both wings at once.
