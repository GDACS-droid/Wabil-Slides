Opening the Chapter · al-Adhkār al-Muwaẓẓafa

Adhkār a Servant Should Never Let Slip

Ibn al-Qayyim titles this closing chapter فِي الأَذْكَارِ المُوَظَّفَةِ الَّتِي لَا يَنْبَغِي لِلْعَبْدِ أَنْ يُخِلَّ بِهَا — the adhkār fixed to their times that a servant ought never to neglect — “and in it are faṣls,” he says, then names them one by one.

Why مُوَظَّفَة — appointed?

وَظِيفَة is a fixed, recurring duty — a thing assigned to its hour. The whole book has argued that remembrance gives the heart its life; the close turns that argument into a shape for the day. Not scattered remembering when one happens to feel like it, but adhkār posted to the dawn, the dusk, the doorway, the bed — so that the believer's day is held, end to end, by the Name of Allah, and no stretch of it is handed over to غَفْلَة.

A corollary

Because these are appointed to times, not to moods, they survive a bad week. The brother who waits to “feel spiritual” before he remembers Allah does it three days a month; the one who has tied سُبْحَانَ اللَّهِ وَبِحَمْدِهِ to the moment he sits down after Fajr does it the morning he is exhausted and the morning he is content alike — because the trigger is the clock and the place, not the heart's weather. That is exactly what muwaẓẓafa is built to do.
