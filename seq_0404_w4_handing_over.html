The Struggle

The Captive Who Gave His Hand

The man who surrendered himself

He follows the sincere adviser once, and once he walks behind the guide of hawā — and the road is cut off against him, his wealth taken, his clothes stripped. Then he asks, “From where was I attacked?” — though he knows the road on which he was robbed, and still refuses any path but that one, because its guide has taken hold of him.

Had he weakened that guide by opposing it, rebuked it when it called, fought it when it tried to seize him, it could never have overpowered him. But he gave it the run of himself; he handed over his own hand. He is like a man who puts his hand into his enemy's hand so the enemy binds him, then torments him cruelly while he cries for help and is not helped. So too the slave: he lets himself be taken captive by shayṭān, hawā, and the nafs al-ammāra — then seeks release and cannot.
