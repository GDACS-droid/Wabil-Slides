The Struggle

The Captive Who Gave His Hand

The man who surrendered himself

He follows the sincere adviser once and it shows him his right way; then once he walks behind the guide of hawā — and the road is cut off against him, his wealth taken, his clothes stripped. Then he asks, “From where was I attacked?” — and the wonder is that he knows the road on which he was robbed, knows the very path where he was waylaid, and still refuses any path but that one, because its guide has taken hold of him and overpowered him.

Had he weakened that guide by opposing it, rebuked it when it called, fought it when it tried to seize him, it could never have mastered him. But he gave it the run of himself; he handed over his own hand (هُوَ أَعْطَاهُ يَدَهُ). He is like a man who places his hand in his enemy's hand so the enemy binds him, then torments him cruelly while he cries for help and is not helped.
A corollary

So the decisive battle is fought early — at the first call, while the guide of hawā is still weak. A habit refused on day one is a stranger easily turned away; indulged for a season, it becomes a captor you obey even while it robs you. The point is not to despair after the surrender but to see where the surrender happens: not at the robbery, but at the handshake.
