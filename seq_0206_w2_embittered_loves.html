Foundation One

The Beloved Itself Is Made Bitter

A settled way of Allah, not a one-off punishment

Ibn al-Qayyim states a سُنَّة — a fixed dealing of Allah with whoever lives this way: أَنْ يُنَكِّدَ عَلَيْهِ مَحَابَّهُ، وَيُنَغِّصَهَا عَلَيْه — “that He embitters his beloved things for him, and spoils them on him.” The very thing the servant preferred to Allah is turned sour in his hand: فَلَا يَنَالُ شَيْئًا مِنْهَا إِلَّا بِنَكَدٍ وَتَنْغِيص — “he attains nothing of it except with vexation and trouble.” This is the recompense, Ibn al-Qayyim says, for preferring his own whim, and the whim of whomever he reveres or loves among creation, over the love of Allah.

The corollary

Notice what this does to a common assumption. We imagine that choosing the dunyā over the command at least delivers the dunyā — that you trade the reward and keep the pleasure. Ibn al-Qayyim says the trade does not even pay out: the pleasure you grabbed at the expense of Allah arrives spoiled, never quite satisfying, always shadowed. The one who keeps love of Allah first does not lose his lawful loves; he is the only one who actually gets to enjoy them, because they have been put in their right place beneath Him.
