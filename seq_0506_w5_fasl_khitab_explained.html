Faṣl al-Khiṭāb

The Decisive Word, Unpacked

Ibn al-Qayyim takes one report — إِنَّ عَبْدِي كُلَّ عَبْدِي الَّذِي يَذْكُرُنِي وَهُوَ مُلَاقٍ قِرْنَهُ, “My true servant, indeed My servant, is the one who remembers Me even as he meets his foe in combat” — as the decisive word (al-faṣl fī al-khiṭāb) in weighing the dhākir against the mujāhid.
  • The warrior who remembers Allah in the fight is above the warrior who fights heedless of Him.
  • The one in dhikr without battle is above the warrior who is heedless of Allah in his battle.
  • So the matter is not “tongue versus sword.” In his words: the best of those who remember are the ones who also strive, and the best of those who strive are the ones who remember.
  • What dhikr adds is the presence of the heart with Allah. The heedless warrior, for all his courage, is fighting with an absent heart; the dhikr is the soul the deed is missing. Take dhikr out of jihād and you have left the body and lost the spirit.

Anchored in the Book

He grounds this in the Qurʾān itself: the command to remember Allah much is given in the very verse about standing firm against the enemy — وَاذْكُرُوا اللَّهَ كَثِيرًا لَّعَلَّكُمْ تُفْلِحُونَ (al-Anfāl 8:45). Even at the point of the sword, falāḥ is tied to remembrance.
